## The problem

The company was paying for Jobber, Mailchimp, Calendly, Google Workspace premium, Dropbox Business, and multiple integration layers. Total recurring spend was about $2,800 per month, and data was spread across six platforms.

The problem was not just cost. Staff were moving information between systems manually because each tool solved only one piece of the workflow.

That meant the stack was expensive in two directions. There was the visible software cost, and then there was the hidden operating cost created by fragmentation. People were paying monthly for convenience while still doing manual coordination work the software was supposed to eliminate.

The business also had a control issue. Core process information was scattered across vendors with different permissions, rules, and pricing models. Even when the tools technically worked, the operating model remained harder to understand than it should have been.

## The approach

We scoped the engagement around infrastructure ownership and reduction of overlapping SaaS spend.

The new stack consolidated file storage, scheduling, analytics, and workflow automation into a private environment. We used self-hosted services where ownership and long-term cost mattered, while keeping only the hosted tools that still made operational sense.

That distinction is important. This was not a “self-host everything” project. The point was to move the expensive, business-critical layers into infrastructure the company could control while avoiding unnecessary churn in places where a hosted tool still made sense.

The architecture work therefore focused on consolidation, not ideology. Every component had to answer a practical question: does this reduce recurring cost, simplify operations, or improve control enough to justify the move?

## The stack

- Nextcloud for file storage
- n8n for workflow automation
- Cal.com for scheduling
- Plausible for privacy-friendly analytics
- Stalwart for email infrastructure
- Cloudflare tunnels for secure access

## Why this worked

The project worked because it addressed both financial waste and system sprawl at the same time. Replacing one subscription in isolation would not have changed much. Replacing several overlapping layers with a coherent operating stack changed how the business understood its own infrastructure.

That is where owned infrastructure becomes valuable. Not because “self-hosted” sounds sophisticated, but because the business can finally see where data lives, how workflows are triggered, and what it is actually paying for.

## The result

In this migration model, monthly tooling cost drops sharply once overlapping subscriptions are removed and the operating stack is consolidated. More importantly, the business no longer has core operating data scattered across six vendors.

The client also gained a cleaner PIPA posture because more of the stack was under direct operational control.

The longer-term gain is predictability. Instead of waiting for vendor pricing changes or feature shifts to reshape the operating budget, the company can make deliberate infrastructure decisions based on business need. That changes the conversation from “what does this vendor allow?” to “what does the business actually need next?”

## Before and after

- Before: six platforms, overlapping subscriptions, fragmented data
- After: controlled stack, fewer recurring invoices, simpler access model
- Timeline: Infrastructure package with phased migration

## Operational takeaway

The main gain was not only cost reduction. The business ended up with a cleaner operating model, clearer data ownership, and a system that could be explained and maintained internally.

That internal explainability matters more than most teams realize. Once the system can be documented clearly, onboarding improves, troubleshooting gets faster, and future improvements stop requiring a full rediscovery of how the business is wired together.

If your software stack costs more every quarter but does not reduce admin work, infrastructure redesign is probably overdue.
